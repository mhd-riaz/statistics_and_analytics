# Formula Cheat Sheet — Sessions 1 to 3

> **How to use this table:** Read the question, scan the **"Keywords to Look For"** column for matching words, then apply the corresponding formula.

---

## Session 1 — Descriptive Statistics

|   #   | Topic                             | Keywords to Look For                                                                                    | Formula                                                                                                     | What to Find                                               | Points to Remember                                                                                                                                 |
| :---: | :-------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | :---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | :--------------------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
|   1   | **Mean (Average)**                | "average", "mean", "typical value", "expected score", "sum divided by count"                            | $$\bar{x} = \frac{\sum x_i}{n}$$                                                                            | A single value representing the center of the data         | Sensitive to **outliers** — one extreme value can drag the mean up or down. Use $\mu$ for population, $\bar{x}$ for sample.                        |
|   2   | **Median**                        | "middle value", "median", "50th percentile", "resistant to outliers", "sorted data"                     | Odd $n$: $x_{\frac{n+1}{2}}$. Even $n$: $\frac{x_{n/2} + x_{n/2+1}}{2}$                                     | The center value when data is sorted                       | **Must sort first.** Better than mean for skewed data (e.g., income). Not affected by outliers.                                                    |
|   3   | **Mode**                          | "most frequent", "mode", "most common", "popular", "occurs the most"                                    | Value with highest frequency $f(x_i)$                                                                       | The value that appears most often                          | A dataset can be **unimodal**, **bimodal**, **multimodal**, or have **no mode** at all. Only measure of central tendency for **categorical** data. |
|   4   | **Range**                         | "range", "spread", "difference between max and min", "highest minus lowest"                             | $$\text{Range} = x_{\max} - x_{\min}$$                                                                      | How far apart the extremes are                             | Highly sensitive to **outliers**. One extreme value inflates the range.                                                                            |
|   5   | **Quartiles (Q1, Q2, Q3)**        | "quartile", "Q1", "Q2", "Q3", "25th percentile", "75th percentile", "divide into four"                  | $L = \frac{k}{100} \times (n+1)$; $Q1 = P_{25}$, $Q2 = P_{50}$, $Q3 = P_{75}$                               | Values that divide sorted data into 4 equal parts          | $Q2$ = median. **Sort data first.** If locator $L$ is not whole, interpolate between neighbours.                                                   |
|   6   | **Interquartile Range (IQR)**     | "IQR", "interquartile", "middle 50%", "outlier detection", "box plot"                                   | $$\text{IQR} = Q3 - Q1$$                                                                                    | Spread of the middle 50% of data                           | Outlier boundaries: below $Q1 - 1.5 \times \text{IQR}$ or above $Q3 + 1.5 \times \text{IQR}$. **Not affected by outliers.**                        |
|   7   | **Variance**                      | "variance", "squared deviation", "spread from mean", "how spread out", "$\sigma^2$", "$s^2$"            | Population: $\sigma^2 = \frac{\sum(x_i - \mu)^2}{n}$. Sample: $s^2 = \frac{\sum(x_i - \bar{x})^2}{n-1}$     | Average of squared deviations from the mean                | **Population** divides by $n$; **Sample** divides by $n-1$ (Bessel's correction). Units are **squared** (e.g., cm²).                               |
|   8   | **Standard Deviation**            | "standard deviation", "SD", "sigma", "spread", "dispersion", "how far from the mean"                    | Population: $\sigma = \sqrt{\sigma^2}$. Sample: $s = \sqrt{s^2}$                                            | Average distance of data points from the mean              | Just the **square root of variance**. Same units as original data. Preferred over variance for interpretation.                                     |
|   9   | **Coefficient of Variation (CV)** | "CV", "coefficient of variation", "relative variability", "compare spread across datasets", "unitless"  | $$CV = \frac{\sigma}{\mu} \times 100\%$$                                                                    | Relative spread as a percentage of the mean                | **Unitless** — use to compare variability between datasets with different scales. Undefined/misleading when mean ≈ 0.                              |
|  10   | **Empirical Rule**                | "68-95-99.7", "empirical rule", "bell curve", "normal distribution", "within 1/2/3 standard deviations" | $\mu \pm 1\sigma$ → ~68%; $\mu \pm 2\sigma$ → ~95%; $\mu \pm 3\sigma$ → ~99.7%                              | Percentage of data within $k$ standard deviations          | **Only for normal distributions.** Do NOT use for skewed/multimodal data.                                                                          |
|  11   | **Chebyshev's Theorem**           | "Chebyshev", "any distribution", "at least", "minimum percentage", "regardless of shape"                | $$\text{At least } \left(1 - \frac{1}{k^2}\right)$$ of data within $k$ SDs                                  | Minimum guaranteed % of data within $k$ SDs                | Works for **ANY** distribution shape. More conservative than Empirical Rule. $k$ must be $> 1$.                                                    |
|  12   | **Correlation (Pearson's $r$)**   | "correlation", "relationship", "linear", "Pearson", "scatter", "direction and strength", "$r$"          | $$r = \frac{\sum(x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{\sqrt{\sum(x_i - \bar{x})^2 \cdot \sum(y_i - \bar{y})^2}}$$ | Strength & direction of linear relationship ($-1$ to $+1$) | Correlation ≠ causation. Only captures **linear** relationships. $r = 0$ doesn't mean "no relationship" — just no **linear** one.                  |

## Session 2 — Probability & Distributions

|   #   | Topic                            | Keywords to Look For                                                                                               | Formula                                                                                       | What to Find                                                   | Points to Remember                                                                                                                                                                                                                           |
| :---: | :------------------------------- | :----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | :-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
|  13   | **Basic Probability**            | "probability", "chance", "likelihood", "favorable outcomes", "possible outcomes"                                   | $$P(A) = \frac{n(A)}{n(S)}$$                                                                  | Likelihood of an event (0 to 1)                                | $P(A) = 0$ → impossible; $P(A) = 1$ → certain. All probabilities in sample space must sum to 1.                                                                                                                                              |
|  14   | **Complement Rule**              | "not", "doesn't happen", "complement", "opposite event", "$A'$"                                                    | $$P(A') = 1 - P(A)$$                                                                          | Probability an event does NOT occur                            | Useful shortcut: sometimes it's easier to find $P(\text{not } A)$ and subtract from 1.                                                                                                                                                       |
|  15   | **Addition Theorem (OR)**        | "or", "either", "union", "at least one of", "$A \cup B$"                                                           | $$P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$$                                                   | Probability of A **or** B (or both)                            | If events are **disjoint** (mutually exclusive): $P(A \cap B) = 0$, so just add. Subtract overlap to avoid double-counting.                                                                                                                  |
|  16   | **Multiplication Theorem (AND)** | "and", "both", "together", "intersection", "joint", "$A \cap B$"                                                   | General: $P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B \mid A)$. Independent: $P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B)$ | Probability of A **and** B both occurring                      | For **dependent** events (e.g., without replacement), use $P(B \mid A)$. For **independent** events, just multiply.                                                                                                                          |
|  17   | **Conditional Probability**      | "given that", "if … already happened", "conditional", "$P(A \mid B)$", "knowing that"                              | $$P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}$$                                                    | Probability of A, knowing B already occurred                   | The "given" **shrinks your sample space** to only outcomes where B happened. $P(B)$ must be $> 0$.                                                                                                                                           |
|  18   | **Law of Total Probability**     | "overall probability", "total probability", "multiple paths/sources/machines", "weighted sum"                      | $$P(B) = \sum P(B \mid A_i) \cdot P(A_i)$$                                                    | Total probability of B across all scenarios                    | Partition must be **mutually exclusive** and **exhaustive**. Think: "split into paths, calculate each, add them up." Usually the **denominator** step for Bayes'.                                                                            |
|  19   | **Bayes' Theorem**               | "Bayes", "reverse", "posterior", "prior", "update belief", "given positive test", "false positive"                 | $$P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(B \mid A) \cdot P(A)}{P(B)}$$                                         | Updated probability (posterior) after new evidence             | **Prior** → your initial belief. **Likelihood** → $P(B \mid A)$. **Evidence** → use Law of Total Probability for $P(B)$. Counter-intuitive results when base rate is low (e.g., rare disease + positive test ≠ high probability of disease). |
|  20   | **Expected Value (Discrete)**    | "expected value", "long-run average", "E(X)", "weighted average of outcomes"                                       | $$E(X) = \sum x_i \cdot P(X = x_i)$$                                                          | The average outcome over many repetitions                      | $E(X)$ may be a value that's **impossible** to actually get (e.g., 3.5 on a die). It's a theoretical long-run average.                                                                                                                       |
|  21   | **Bernoulli Distribution**       | "single trial", "yes/no", "success/failure", "one attempt", "two outcomes", "pass/fail"                            | $$P(X = x) = p^x \cdot q^{1-x}$$; $E(X) = p$; $\text{Var}(X) = pq$                            | Probability of success or failure on **one** trial             | $x \in \{0, 1\}$ only. $q = 1 - p$. Building block of the Binomial.                                                                                                                                                                          |
|  22   | **Binomial Distribution**        | "out of $n$ trials", "$n$ choose $k$", "how many successes", "fixed number of trials", "independently"             | $$P(X = k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k q^{n-k}$$; $E(X) = np$; $\text{Var}(X) = npq$                   | Probability of exactly $k$ successes in $n$ independent trials | Each trial must be **independent** with the **same** $p$. "At least" / "at most" → sum multiple $P(X=k)$ values. $\binom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}$.                                                                                       |
|  23   | **Poisson Distribution**         | "average rate", "per hour/day/month", "lambda", "$\lambda$", "events in an interval", "how many in a fixed period" | $$P(X = k) = \frac{e^{-\lambda} \lambda^k}{k!}$$; $E(X) = \lambda$; $\text{Var}(X) = \lambda$ | Probability of exactly $k$ events in a fixed interval          | Mean = Variance = $\lambda$. Events must be **independent** and at a **constant rate**. No fixed number of trials — just a known rate.                                                                                                       |
|  24   | **Normal Distribution**          | "bell curve", "normal", "Gaussian", "symmetric", "mean and standard deviation describe it"                         | $$f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\right)^2}$$    | The probability density at any value $x$                       | Mean = Median = Mode. Fully described by $\mu$ and $\sigma$. Total area = 1. Convert to Z-score to look up probabilities.                                                                                                                    |

## Session 3 — Inferential Statistics

|   #   | Topic                                      | Keywords to Look For                                                                                                 | Formula                                                                                        | What to Find                                                    | Points to Remember                                                                                                                                                   |
| :---: | :----------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | :--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | :-------------------------------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
|  25   | **Z-Score**                                | "z-score", "how many standard deviations", "standardize", "how unusual", "above/below average"                       | Population: $Z = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma}$. Sample: $Z = \frac{x - \bar{x}}{s}$                  | How many SDs a value is from the mean                           | $Z = 0$ → at the mean. $\|Z\| > 3$ → likely an outlier. Converts any normal distribution to the **Standard Normal** ($\mu=0$, $\sigma=1$).                           |
|  26   | **CDF (Cumulative Distribution Function)** | "or less", "at most", "cumulative", "up to", "$F(x)$", "$P(X \le x)$"                                                | Discrete: $F(x) = \sum_{x_i \le x} P(X = x_i)$. Continuous (uniform): $F(x) = \frac{x-a}{b-a}$ | Probability that $X$ is **less than or equal to** a given value | CDF **never decreases** — only flat or goes up. Starts at 0, ends at 1. For a range: $P(a < X \le b) = F(b) - F(a)$.                                                 |
|  27   | **Standard Error (SE)**                    | "standard error", "SE", "precision of the mean", "how reliable is the average", "spread of sample means"             | $$SE = \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}}$$                                                                    | How much the sample mean bounces around the true mean           | Different from SD! SD = spread of **individual values**. SE = spread of **sample means**. SE gets **smaller** as $n$ increases.                                      |
|  28   | **Sampling Distribution & CLT**            | "Central Limit Theorem", "CLT", "sampling distribution", "many samples", "sample size ≥ 30", "distribution of means" | $\mu_{\bar{x}} = \mu$; $\sigma_{\bar{x}} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$                            | The mean and spread of the distribution of sample means         | CLT says: sample means → **normal distribution**, regardless of population shape, when $n \ge 30$. Larger $n$ → narrower bell curve → more precise estimates.        |
|  29   | **Point Estimate**                         | "best guess", "single number estimate", "point estimate", "sample proportion", "$\hat{p}$", "estimate the mean"      | Mean: $\bar{x} = \frac{\sum x_i}{n}$. Proportion: $\hat{p} = \frac{x}{n}$                      | A single-number best guess for a population parameter           | Simple but gives **no sense of uncertainty**. That's why we also need interval estimates. $\bar{x}$ estimates $\mu$; $\hat{p}$ estimates $p$. Both are **unbiased**. |
|  30   | **Confidence Interval**                    | "confidence interval", "CI", "margin of error", "range of values", "between … and …", "± ", "95% confident"          | $$CI = \bar{x} \pm Z \times \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$$                                          | A range that likely contains the true population parameter      | Common Z-values: **90%** → 1.645, **95%** → 1.960, **99%** → 2.576. Higher confidence → **wider** interval. Larger $n$ → **narrower** interval.                      |
|  31   | **Confidence vs Significance Level**       | "alpha", "$\alpha$", "significance level", "confidence level", "risk of error", "Type I error"                       | $$C = 1 - \alpha$$                                                                             | The relationship between confidence and error risk              | $\alpha = 0.05$ means 5% chance of false positive (Type I error). **Choose $\alpha$ before** the experiment. Higher confidence = stricter threshold = wider CI.      |

## Quick Decision Flowchart

```
  Question asks about...
  │
  ├── CENTER of data? ──────────── Mean / Median / Mode
  │    ├── "average" → Mean
  │    ├── "middle value" / skewed data → Median
  │    └── "most frequent" → Mode
  │
  ├── SPREAD of data? ──────────── Range / IQR / Variance / SD / CV
  │    ├── "max minus min" → Range
  │    ├── "middle 50%" / outlier detection → IQR
  │    ├── "how spread" (in squared units) → Variance
  │    ├── "how spread" (in same units) → Standard Deviation
  │    └── "compare spread across datasets" → CV
  │
  ├── DISTRIBUTION rules? ──────── Empirical Rule / Chebyshev
  │    ├── "normal distribution" + "within k SD" → Empirical Rule
  │    └── "any distribution" + "at least" → Chebyshev
  │
  ├── PROBABILITY of event? ────── Basic / Complement / Addition / Multiplication
  │    ├── "what is the chance of A" → P(A) = n(A)/n(S)
  │    ├── "not A" → Complement
  │    ├── "A or B" → Addition Theorem
  │    └── "A and B" → Multiplication Theorem
  │
  ├── CONDITIONAL / UPDATED? ───── Conditional Prob / Bayes
  │    ├── "given that B happened" → Conditional Probability
  │    ├── "overall probability from multiple paths" → Law of Total Probability
  │    └── "reverse the given / update belief" → Bayes' Theorem
  │
  ├── HOW MANY successes/events? ── Bernoulli / Binomial / Poisson
  │    ├── "single trial, yes/no" → Bernoulli
  │    ├── "k successes out of n trials" → Binomial
  │    └── "events per interval, given a rate λ" → Poisson
  │
  ├── HOW UNUSUAL is a value? ──── Z-Score
  │    └── "how many SDs from mean" → Z = (x − μ) / σ
  │
  ├── CUMULATIVE probability? ──── CDF
  │    └── "x or less / at most x" → F(x)
  │
  └── ESTIMATE population? ──────── Point Estimate / Confidence Interval
       ├── "single best guess" → Point Estimate (x̄ or p̂)
       ├── "range with confidence" → Confidence Interval
       └── "how reliable is the mean" → Standard Error
```
